Integrating expert knowledge into small models for financial-crime detection

Researchers: Dr. Lindeberg Pessoa Leite

Financial crime is among the criminal activities that move the largest volumes of money, making it a central priority for law-enforcement agencies and financial-intelligence units. In this context, the Bank Transaction Investigation System (SIMBA) serves as the reference setting: SIMBA is the Brazilian bank-transaction investigation standard that organizes the secure transfer of banking data between financial institutions and public bodies after judicial authorization to lift bank secrecy.

In this project, the method is structured into three steps:

  1. Step 1 — Synthetic SIMBA. Due to the sensitivity of real records, all experiments are conducted on a simulator calibrated to produce SIMBA-like csv data (Altman et al., 2023). On top of these files, a compact set of fraudulent typologies (e.g., structuring/smurfing, pass-through chains, layering) is programmatically injected to obtain labelled examples.
  2. Step 2 — Knowledge Graph and Provenance. From the SIMBA csv files, a knowledge graph (KG) of accounts, people, agencies, and relations is constructed. The KG resolves identities, supports incremental updates, exposes multi-hop structures for visualization, and maintains pointers to original SIMBA rows and keys. Model outputs (Step 3) are attached to KG nodes and edges, enabling end-to-end inspection from each decision back to the csv records (Rashkin et al., 2023).
  3. Step 3 — Pattern adapters. Using KG-derived features, a base graph neural network is first trained on fraud-versus-non-fraud labels to capture structural and statistical patterns of criminal behaviour. The encoder is then frozen and, for each fraud typology, a lightweight adapter and classification head are attached. Adapter activations are recorded as pattern-specific signals on the KG, allowing each decision to be decomposed into contributing patterns and traced back to the corresponding nodes, edges, and csv rows (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Nature StructIE, 2024; Li et al., 2024).
